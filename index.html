After death, something may eventually happen subjectively — but is experience impersonal?

Yajnavalkya, the great sage of the Brihadaranyaka Upanishad, was about to leave home. His wife Maitreyi stopped him with one question: "If the whole earth filled with wealth were mine, would I be immortal?" He said simply: "No." Then she asked the question that changed everything — "Then what shall I do with wealth? Teach me what you know." He said: "As a lump of salt has neither inside nor outside and is altogether a homogeneous mass of taste — even so the Self has neither inside nor outside. It is altogether a homogeneous mass of Intelligence." Krishnamurti said the same thing differently: the ending of the self is not death — it is the beginning of something entirely new. The salt does not grieve becoming the ocean. It is only the idea of being a lump that grieves.

What does quantum physics actually say about consciousness?

The observer effect in quantum mechanics raises a question that won't sit quietly: does the act of observation change what is observed? Planck said in 1931 — "I regard consciousness as fundamental. I regard matter as derivative from consciousness." This is not mysticism. It is the father of quantum theory.

Why did ancient monks know what neuroscientists just discovered?

In September 2002, Tibetan monk Mingyur Rinpoche walked into a brain lab in Wisconsin. When scientists studied his brain alongside 20 other long-term meditators, they found elevated gamma oscillations not just during meditation — but in their everyday baseline brain activity. The monks had been describing this state for 2,500 years.

What is the science of prayer — and does it actually work?

Harvard's Ted Kaptchuk found that patients who knew they were taking placebos reported twice the symptom relief of the no-treatment group. The Princeton PEAR lab ran over 1,000 experimental series over 12 years and found statistically significant deviations from chance when humans intended to influence random outputs. We are not separate from what we observe.

What happens to the brain when you chant a mantra?

An fMRI study found significant bilateral deactivation of the amygdala, hippocampus, and orbitofrontal cortex during OM chanting — while chanting "ssss" produced no effect. Researchers noted this pattern mirrored vagus nerve stimulation used medically for depression and epilepsy. Ancient sages prescribed what modern medicine is only discovering.

Near-death experiences: what does the science actually say?

The AWARE II study followed 567 cardiac arrest patients across the US and UK. Survivors reported powerful, lucid experiences — separation from the body, life reviews, encounters with light — distinct from hallucinations or dreams. EEG recordings showed unexpected brain electrical activity long into CPR. The data is real. The explanation is still open.

If we are made of atoms that never touch, why do we feel the touch of others?

What we perceive as touch is the brain's interpretation of electromagnetic forces between electron clouds — atoms never actually meet. The Vedas called this Maya: not illusion, but constructed reality. Ma means "to construct." The separation you feel is real as experience. It is not real as substance. Not two things meeting — one thing, remembering.

Can bigger things remain invisible to human eyes? What does science say?

Everything ever seen — every star, mountain, face, and galaxy — makes up less than 5% of the universe. Dark matter makes up 27% of everything and is invisible to every instrument humanity has built. Dark energy is the force driving the accelerated expansion of the cosmos. The visible is the minority report. The Vedas called the invisible Akasha — the ground of all form.

Vedic Wisdom

How do ancient Indian texts explain the origin of time, space and matter?

The Nasadiya Sukta of the Rig Veda begins: "Then, there was neither existence nor non-existence." It asks when, why, and through whom the universe came into being — and concludes that even the gods may not know, for they came after creation. Carl Sagan wrote that Hinduism is the only religion whose time scales correspond to those of modern scientific cosmology — cycles of 8.64 billion years.

Did the Puranas describe time dilation 2,500 years before Einstein?

King Kakudmi and his daughter Revati visited Brahmaloka for what felt like a brief audience with Brahma. When they returned, 27 Chaturyugas had passed on Earth — over 116 million years. King Muchukunda slept in a cave while ages turned, and woke in a world where his entire dynasty had dissolved into history. The Bhagavata Purana was describing gravitational time dilation in story form.
